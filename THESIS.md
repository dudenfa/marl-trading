# THESIS: Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning In A Synthetic Spot Market

## 1. Project Context

This repository is the second major phase of my university thesis.

The first phase was developed in the sibling repository `algo-trading-drl`, where the focus was single-agent Reinforcement Learning for intraday trading on real QQQ limit order book data. That work produced an important result: even after substantial improvements in environment design, reward shaping, features, validation, evaluation, and training methodology, the remaining limitations appeared to be structural rather than purely infrastructural.

Because of that, the next step of the thesis is not simply to keep refining a single trader on historical data. Instead, the goal is to move toward a synthetic market in which the market itself is generated by the interaction of multiple agents. In this new setting, price, liquidity, volatility, and trading opportunities are not fixed by historical data alone, but emerge from the behavior of the participants.

This shift is important both scientifically and conceptually:

- the first phase asked whether a single RL agent could learn sensible trading behavior from a historical market,
- the second phase asks what kinds of market behavior emerge when many agents interact inside a synthetic exchange and affect one another directly.

This repository is therefore dedicated to a new thesis direction based on Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL), synthetic market simulation, and emergent market behavior.

## 2. Core Thesis Idea

The main thesis question is:

> In a synthetic market where only agents trade and their actions directly influence price, liquidity, and volatility, what behaviors, regimes, and strategic dynamics can emerge and be studied?

This means that the thesis is not primarily about building the most profitable trader possible.

Instead, it is about studying:

- how markets can form from interacting agents,
- how liquidity appears and disappears,
- how volatility emerges from order flow,
- how different agent types affect one another,
- how information asymmetry changes behavior,
- how large and small participants behave in the same market,
- whether coordination, imitation, crowding, or predatory behavior emerges,
- and how a synthetic market reacts to shocks, news, and structural imbalance.

The emphasis of this thesis should therefore be:

- interpretability,
- market ecology,
- emergent behavior,
- experimental control,
- and visual analysis.

Profitability can still be measured, but it is not the main scientific objective of the project.

## 3. Why MARL Is The Right Direction

In real financial markets, price does not move in isolation. It moves because many participants submit orders, provide liquidity, react to one another, process information differently, and compete for execution and profit.

A single-agent RL setup has a fundamental limitation: the market is exogenous. The agent reacts to a world it does not create.

In the new MARL setup, the market becomes endogenous:

- agents create order flow,
- order flow changes the order book,
- the order book changes execution conditions,
- execution conditions change agent behavior,
- and those new behaviors feed back into the market.

This creates a much richer and more realistic research setting for studying strategic behavior.

The scientific advantage of this new direction is not that it is automatically more realistic than the previous work. The advantage is that it allows the thesis to study questions that cannot even be asked in a single-agent historical setup, such as:

- what happens when liquidity providers and liquidity takers coexist,
- how information asymmetry shapes price discovery,
- how heterogeneous capital sizes influence the market,
- whether the market self-stabilizes or becomes unstable,
- and whether agent populations produce coherent regimes such as calm markets, trend phases, panic, or liquidity droughts.

## 4. General Philosophy Of The New Project

The new project should be built with the same methodological discipline that guided the best part of the first thesis phase:

- define the problem clearly before optimizing,
- prefer interpretable systems over unnecessarily complex ones,
- introduce one important change at a time,
- keep the first version stable and understandable,
- and only then move toward richer MARL experiments.

For this reason, the first version of this repository should not start with a fully general, maximally realistic, many-learning-agent market.

That would be too difficult to debug, too hard to interpret, and too risky for a thesis.

Instead, the project should start with a stylized but serious synthetic market:

- one asset,
- spot-only,
- event-driven,
- limit-order-book based,
- heterogeneous fixed agent types,
- no direct communication,
- full public observability of market activity,
- and strong visual replay tools.

The goal of the first prototype is not to train the final MARL system immediately.

The goal of the first prototype is:

- to create a stable and coherent market world,
- to verify that the market exhibits believable dynamics,
- to measure and visualize those dynamics,
- and to create a foundation on top of which RL agents can later be introduced.

## 5. High-Level Research Framing

The new thesis can be framed as a study of synthetic market ecology.

The market will be entirely simulated. It will not replay historical QQQ data. However, it will still contain economically meaningful structure:

- a tradable asset,
- a central limit order book,
- heterogeneous participants,
- hidden fundamentals,
- public market events,
- noisy information,
- execution constraints,
- and portfolio-based failure.

This lets the thesis investigate how the interaction of agent types can generate:

- price discovery,
- market impact,
- spread dynamics,
- liquidity concentration,
- volatility clustering,
- order flow imbalance,
- crowding,
- speculative behavior,
- and responses to shocks.

This is especially useful because the thesis claim becomes more precise:

- not “this is how real QQQ definitely behaves,”
- but “in a controlled synthetic market with these mechanisms, these kinds of behaviors emerge.”

That is a strong and defensible scientific position.

## 6. Locked Decisions For MARL V1

The following decisions were agreed during project design discussions and should be treated as the starting specification for MARL V1.

### Market Structure

- The market will have one asset only in the first version.
- The market will be a synthetic market, not calibrated as a direct replay of QQQ.
- The market should conceptually behave like a 24/7 market rather than a traditional exchange with fixed opening and closing hours.
- Even though the market is conceptually continuous, training and simulation will still use finite episodes for practical reasons.

### Trading Rules

- The first market will be spot-only.
- No leverage in V1.
- No short selling in V1.
- Agents will hold only cash and the asset.
- Portfolio constraints should matter directly for what orders an agent is allowed to place.

### Failure / Ruin

- Agents can fail if their equity drops too far below the starting level.
- Ruin thresholds should be configurable per agent type.
- When an agent falls below its ruin threshold, all of its resting orders are canceled and the agent is deactivated for the rest of the episode.

### Exchange Visibility

- Agents cannot directly communicate with one another.
- The environment should behave in a blockchain-like public way:
  - agents do not send messages to one another,
  - but they can see what others are doing through the public market.
- Agent identities should remain stable across the whole episode, similar to wallet addresses or persistent public trader IDs.
- All agents should see the full order book in V1.

### Information Structure

- The market should contain a hidden latent fundamental value process.
- The market should also include public news events that can shift beliefs, volume, and price behavior.
- Some agents should receive noisy private signals related to fundamentals or news.
- Other agents should rely only on the public tape and public order book.

### Agent Population

- The first version should use fixed heterogeneous agent types.
- The very first prototype should contain zero RL agents.
- Immediately after the basic market is stable, the next step should be to add one RL agent into the existing scripted market ecology.
- A large whale-style agent should not be included in the very first prototype.
- Whale or institution-like agents can be added later once the baseline market is stable and interpretable.

### Exchange Engine

- The market should be event-driven rather than synchronous tick-based.
- Limit orders are important and should be part of the first version.
- The first version should include:
  - limit orders,
  - market orders,
  - cancellations,
  - and a central limit order book.

### Order Capacity Constraint

- Agents should not be allowed to place unlimited resting orders.
- Order capacity should be constrained by portfolio resources.
- Example principle:
  - if an agent has limited cash and inventory, it should not be able to place buy and sell orders whose combined commitments exceed what that portfolio can support.

This resource-constrained order placement is important because it prevents unrealistic infinite-liquidity behavior and ties market presence to balance-sheet reality.

## 7. MARL V1 Scientific Objective

The objective of MARL V1 is:

> Build a stable, interpretable, synthetic one-asset spot market with heterogeneous agents and a public order book, then study the behaviors that emerge from interaction.

The first milestone is therefore not “train a profitable MARL strategy.”

The first milestone is:

- a functioning exchange,
- a functioning synthetic market,
- coherent agent participation,
- measurable liquidity and price formation,
- and visual tools to inspect what is happening.

The first strong research milestone after that is:

> Introduce one RL agent into the scripted ecology and study how it behaves in a market generated by other agents.

Only after that should the project move toward multiple learning agents and full MARL experiments.

## 8. Why We Do Not Start With Fully Learning Agents

Starting with all-learning agents in a synthetic market may sound conceptually pure, but it is not the best first step for a thesis.

If every agent learns from scratch at the same time, several problems appear:

- the market may fail to form coherent liquidity,
- price may become arbitrary or unstable,
- training may become highly non-stationary,
- debugging becomes much harder,
- and it becomes difficult to tell whether a behavior is meaningful or just an artifact of bad initial conditions.

A mixed ecology solves this problem.

Scripted agents can provide:

- baseline liquidity,
- diverse trading styles,
- interpretable behavior,
- and a stable environment in which the first RL agent can be studied.

This does not make the project less interesting. On the contrary, it makes the early stages much more scientific because each new layer of complexity can be introduced in a controlled way.

## 9. Market World Design

### 9.1 One Asset

The first version will use one synthetic asset only.

This keeps the system focused and avoids multi-asset complexity such as cross-asset arbitrage, portfolio allocation, and contagion between markets.

One asset is enough to study:

- price formation,
- spread behavior,
- volatility,
- liquidity,
- order flow,
- and strategic interaction.

### 9.2 Spot-Only Market

The first version should feel similar in spirit to an early crypto-like market:

- always on,
- no leverage,
- no borrowing,
- no short selling,
- and direct ownership of cash plus asset.

This has several advantages:

- simpler accounting,
- fewer edge cases,
- easier interpretation,
- and better alignment with the first synthetic-market prototype.

Leverage and short selling are interesting future extensions, but they should be postponed until the base market is working well.

### 9.3 Event-Driven Exchange

The exchange should be event-driven.

That means the market evolves one event at a time, for example:

- an order submission,
- an order cancellation,
- a trade execution,
- a news shock,
- or an internal clock advance.

This is preferable to a fully synchronous “all agents act at once each tick” model because:

- it feels more like an exchange,
- it produces a more natural order stream,
- it handles asynchronous behavior more cleanly,
- and it supports realistic queue and matching logic.

### 9.4 Central Limit Order Book

The exchange engine should use a central limit order book with price-time priority.

At minimum, V1 should support:

- market buy orders,
- market sell orders,
- limit buy orders,
- limit sell orders,
- and order cancellation.

This is enough to generate:

- spread,
- depth,
- price impact,
- queue competition,
- and endogenous liquidity.

## 10. Fundamental Value, News, And Information

The market should not be a pure random-walk toy without economic structure.

Instead, V1 should include three layers of information:

### 10.1 Latent Fundamental Value

There should be a hidden fundamental value process `F_t`.

This process is not directly visible to all agents. It serves as the hidden anchor of the asset’s underlying worth.

Possible properties of `F_t`:

- slow drift,
- stochastic noise,
- regime changes,
- and discrete jumps.

This is useful because it gives the market a meaningful hidden structure. It creates a difference between:

- observed market price,
- and hidden underlying value.

That gap is what allows price discovery, mispricing, informed trading, and overreaction to exist.

### 10.2 Public News Events

The market should also include public news events.

These events may:

- shift the latent fundamental,
- increase uncertainty,
- increase order arrival intensity,
- or change the behavior of some agent classes.

Public news is important because it creates:

- bursts of volume,
- jumps in volatility,
- and visible changes in market state.

It also makes the market easier to interpret visually and narratively.

### 10.3 Noisy Private Signals

Some agents should receive noisy private information related to the latent fundamental or news.

This allows the market to contain information asymmetry without requiring explicit communication.

That means:

- some agents are better informed,
- others infer information only from public price and order flow,
- and the market becomes a place where information is gradually revealed through interaction.

This is one of the most important ingredients for generating rich strategic behavior.

## 11. Public Observability And Blockchain-Like Design

The public market should work in a blockchain-like way:

- actions are publicly visible through market activity,
- agent identities persist across time,
- agents cannot talk directly,
- but they can react to what they observe others doing.

The visible public information should include:

- the full order book,
- recent trades,
- trade prices and sizes,
- which public agent ID submitted or canceled an order when that is intended to be visible in the design,
- and basic market state statistics.

This gives agents a way to condition on the behavior of others without building an explicit communication protocol in V1.

That is important because it allows the thesis to study:

- imitation,
- chasing,
- crowding,
- predation,
- and public-behavior-driven coordination,

without adding a separate messaging layer too early.

## 12. Agent Ecology For V1

The initial agent population should be fixed and heterogeneous.

The first ecology should include the following broad types:

### 12.1 Market Makers

Purpose:

- provide two-sided liquidity,
- earn spread,
- stabilize short-term book depth,
- and react to inventory pressure.

Typical behavior:

- place buy and sell limit orders near the current mid or reference price,
- manage inventory carefully,
- widen spreads under stress,
- cancel and replace orders as the market moves.

### 12.2 Noise / Retail Traders

Purpose:

- generate uninformed order flow,
- create background volume,
- and produce realistic randomness in the market.

Typical behavior:

- small market or limit orders,
- random timing,
- weak directional biases,
- simple reactions to recent moves or public signals.

### 12.3 Trend Followers

Purpose:

- amplify short-term or medium-term trends,
- create persistence,
- and contribute to momentum-like phases.

Typical behavior:

- buy after upward public signals,
- sell or reduce exposure after downward signals,
- submit orders more aggressively when the market appears to move strongly.

### 12.4 Value / Informed Traders

Purpose:

- trade on perceived mispricing relative to the hidden fundamental,
- push price back toward value,
- and create price discovery.

Typical behavior:

- use noisy private signals,
- buy when the asset appears cheap relative to estimated value,
- sell when it appears expensive,
- provide directional order flow with information content.

### 12.5 Large Whale / Institution-Like Agents

Purpose:

- introduce large footprint behavior,
- produce visible market impact,
- and create the possibility of liquidity shocks or crowd reactions.

Status in V1:

- not included in the very first prototype,
- to be added later once the baseline market is stable.

This staged approach is important because whale behavior is interesting, but it would make debugging much harder if added immediately.

## 13. Portfolio And Constraint Model

Each agent should have:

- cash,
- asset inventory,
- total equity,
- a ruin threshold,
- and a cap on how much outstanding order commitment it can maintain.

Since the market is spot-only:

- buy orders require sufficient available cash,
- sell orders require sufficient available asset inventory,
- and resting orders should reserve the necessary resources.

This is very important.

The order book should not be populated by unrealistic orders that the agent cannot actually finance.

A useful resource-accounting rule is:

- cash reserved by resting buy orders cannot be reused,
- asset reserved by resting sell orders cannot be reused,
- total outstanding exposure must remain feasible given the portfolio.

This directly ties liquidity provision to actual balance-sheet capacity.

## 14. Ruin And Deactivation

Ruin is not margin liquidation in the leveraged sense, because V1 is spot-only.

Instead, ruin should be treated as portfolio failure.

Example principle:

- an agent begins with `10,000`,
- if its equity drops below a configured threshold, such as `4,000`,
- it is considered functionally out of the game.

When that happens:

- all of its resting orders are canceled,
- it is deactivated,
- and it cannot submit new orders for the rest of the episode.

This is a simple but powerful mechanism because it creates:

- survival pressure,
- asymmetric outcomes across agents,
- and an interpretable definition of failure.

It also makes long episodes more meaningful by allowing the population itself to evolve within the episode.

## 15. Episode Design

Even though the market is conceptually 24/7, the simulator still needs finite episodes.

The best first approach is to define an episode not as a traditional trading day, but as a fixed simulation horizon.

Possible options:

- a fixed number of exchange events,
- a fixed number of agent decisions,
- or a fixed amount of simulated market time.

This preserves the idea of a non-stop market while still making training and analysis manageable.

A good default principle for V1 is:

- treat the market as continuous,
- but run episodes over finite horizons long enough to observe regime changes, news events, and evolving agent behavior.

## 16. Why Visual Replay Is Essential

Visual replay is a core requirement of this project, not a secondary feature.

The thesis must make it possible to literally see the market behavior.

Metrics alone are not enough.

The system should support visual inspection of:

- price path,
- latent fundamental path,
- spread evolution,
- book depth,
- volume bursts,
- per-agent cash and inventory,
- per-agent equity,
- order placements and cancellations,
- trades,
- agent activation and ruin,
- and the sequence of events around news shocks.

Visual replay is especially important because the thesis is about emergent behavior. Emergent behavior is often much easier to understand visually than through summary tables alone.

## 17. Metrics To Track In MARL V1

The first version should produce both market-level metrics and agent-level metrics.

### 17.1 Market-Level Metrics

- midpoint price path
- latent fundamental path
- spread statistics
- order book depth statistics
- traded volume
- volatility
- return distribution
- liquidity drought frequency
- order imbalance
- trade sign imbalance
- shock response metrics

### 17.2 Agent-Level Metrics

- cash
- inventory
- equity
- realized and unrealized PnL
- number of resting orders
- submission and cancellation counts
- trade participation
- aggressor vs passive fills
- survival / ruin status
- time active in the market

### 17.3 Ecology-Level Metrics

- fraction of volume by agent type
- fraction of liquidity by agent type
- price impact by agent type
- concentration of inventory
- concentration of wealth
- response heterogeneity after news
- population turnover through deactivation

## 18. Engineering Roadmap

The project should be developed in stages.

### Stage 1: Exchange Prototype

Build the basic event-driven spot exchange:

- central limit order book
- price-time priority
- order submission
- cancellation
- execution
- portfolio accounting
- order reservation logic

Goal:

- verify that the exchange mechanics are correct and stable.

### Stage 2: Scripted Market Ecology

Add the fixed heterogeneous agent population:

- market makers
- noise traders
- trend followers
- value/informed traders

Add:

- latent fundamental process
- news events
- noisy private signals
- public order book visibility
- stable public agent identities
- ruin and deactivation

Goal:

- create a functioning synthetic market with interpretable behavior.

### Stage 3: Analysis And Replay

Add:

- visual event replay
- market charts
- agent-level dashboards
- event logs
- shock analysis

Goal:

- make the market inspectable and thesis-ready.

### Stage 4: First RL Integration

Introduce one RL agent into the scripted ecology.

Goal:

- study how a learning agent behaves in a stable synthetic market created by other agents.

### Stage 5: Multi-RL Extensions

Add:

- more than one RL agent
- stronger heterogeneity in capital and objectives
- whale-style agents
- richer strategic interaction

Goal:

- move from market construction to true MARL experimentation.

## 19. Research Questions For Later Phases

Once the V1 market is stable, the following thesis questions become natural:

- How do informed and uninformed agents jointly shape price discovery?
- Under what conditions does the market become volatile or illiquid?
- Does public visibility of agent identities create imitation or predatory behavior?
- What happens when a large agent enters a market originally dominated by small traders?
- Do some agent ecologies self-stabilize while others self-destabilize?
- How do news shocks propagate through the order book?
- Does an RL agent learn to provide liquidity, consume liquidity, or exploit the behavior of specific agent classes?
- Do multiple learning agents create new kinds of regimes that do not appear in the scripted ecology alone?

## 20. What MARL V1 Is Not

To keep the project focused, MARL V1 should not attempt all of the following at once:

- no multi-asset portfolio world,
- no leverage,
- no short selling,
- no direct communication channel,
- no full realism of modern equity exchanges,
- no whale in the first prototype,
- no immediate all-learning-agent world.

These are not rejected forever.

They are simply postponed in order to protect clarity, stability, and interpretability.

## 21. Main Expected Contribution Of This Repository

The strongest contribution of this project should be:

- a rigorous synthetic market simulator,
- a structured agent ecology,
- a clear framework for studying emergent market behavior,
- and a progressive path from scripted multi-agent dynamics to MARL.

If this is done well, the thesis contribution will not just be “I trained another trading model.”

It will be:

> I designed a synthetic market environment in which price, liquidity, and volatility emerge from heterogeneous agents interacting through a public limit order book, and I used that environment to study strategic behavior and market dynamics under controlled conditions.

That is a much more interesting and ambitious thesis direction.

## 22. Immediate Next Development Goal

The immediate next goal for this repository should be:

> Implement MARL V1 as a stable event-driven one-asset spot market with a central limit order book, fixed heterogeneous scripted agents, hidden fundamentals, public news, noisy private signals, public market observability, portfolio-based order constraints, and agent deactivation on ruin.
